@ABSTRACT

The proliferation of LLMs in various fields has created an unprecedented need to explore the optimization of performance with the aid of prompt engineering strategies. Although the structural influence of chain-of-thought and few-shot prompting has been widely studied, the impact of the pragmatic tone, i.e., the degree of rudeness or politeness, has not been sufficiently explored. This paper presents the results of a meta-analytic examination of the impact of the tone of the prompts on the quality of the outputs generated by LLMs, considering various models (GPT-4o, GPT-4o mini, Gemini 2.0 Flash, LLaMA-4 Scout, LLaMA-2 70B), languages (English, Chinese, Japanese), and task domains (STEM, Humanities, summarization). Based on the results of recent empirical studies, the findings are synthesized under a unified perspective, and the Tone Sensitivity Alignment Hypothesis (TSAH) is developed, which proposes the influence of tone on the performance of LLMs can be explained by three factors: the alignment of the training data distribution, reinforcement learning from human feedback, and cultural linguistic norms. The results indicate the tone of the prompts has a significant impact on the performance of LLMs, and the impact is nonlinear and dependent on the context. For generative and interpretive tasks, the optimal performance is achieved with moderate levels of politeness, and extremely rude prompts lead to a drop in the accuracy of the results by 3-8% in the Humanities domain. Moreover, the results indicate newer models are less sensitive to tone, and the impact diminishes with the aggregation of the results on the domain level.

# Introduction

Large Language Models (LLMs) have been recognized as game-changing technologies with significant potential to revolutionize the field of natural language processing, covering the gamut of text generation, summarization, and reasoning, and decision-making capabilities \cite{ref1}. As these models start to be integrated into the production environment in the fields of medicine, law, and education, and the creative arts, understanding the factors which influence the quality of their outputs has become a pressing concern. Prompt engineering, the process of designing the input query to the model, has therefore become an important area of research in the field of natural language processing \cite{ref2}.

Most of the research in prompt engineering has focused on structural and cognitive scaffolding methods. Chain of thought (CoT) prompting requires models to solve problems in a step-by-step fashion and results in significant performance gains on arithmetic and common sense tasks \cite{ref3}. Few-shot prompting offers examples to help models interpret tasks without fine-tuning. More recent work in self-consistency prompting offers multiple reasoning pathways and selects the most frequent answer, showing promise in increasing robustness compared to previous methods that require a chain of thought \cite{ref5}. All of these methods combined answer the "what" of prompt engineering.

Nevertheless, an orthogonal and hitherto largely ignored factor relates to the how of prompt construction, including the pragmatic tone, register, and social connotations of the underlying query. With respect to human communication, the degree of politeness involved in the formulation of the query has been shown to have a significant effect on the extent of compliance, quality of cooperation, and degree of engaged response \cite{ref6}. Given the vast corpus of human-written text upon which LLMs are trained, it is reasonable to propose the following hypothesis.

Recent empirical research has started to support this claim. Yin et al. \cite{ref7} carried out a cross-lingual experiment across English, Chinese, and Japanese languages, confirming that impolite prompts tend to harm the performance of LLMs, while overly polite prompts do not guarantee better performance. Dobariya and Kumar \cite{ref8} found an interesting reversal of results for GPT-4o, where rude prompts were found to outperform polite ones, with accuracy between 80.8% for very polite and 84.8% for very rude prompts. A further study by Cai et al. \cite{ref9} extended this investigation to three different families of models, namely GPT-4o mini, Gemini 2.0 Flash, and LLaMA-4 Scout, revealing tone-dependent, domain-specific, and Humanities-task-specific results. Other research into emotional prompting has found this to improve the performance of LLMs by between 8% and 115% \cite{ref10}, while other research into sentiment variant analysis has found negative prompts to harm factual accuracy and increase bias \cite{ref11}.

Although these findings, individually, are interesting and useful, they offer contradictory results and no overarching framework for understanding the phenomena being studied. The current work attempts to fill the existing gap by providing a synthetic overview of the existing literature and introducing a new model.

## Problem Statement

While there is increasing support for the fact that tone plays a significant role in LLM performance, there is still limited knowledge on the conditions and reasons for which tone influences LLM performance. Current research is limited to individual models, individual languages, and individual domains, leading to apparently conflicting results: polite prompts are sometimes shown to improve model performance and sometimes to reduce performance. There is no framework to account for these findings and provide guidelines for prompt construction.

## Contributions

The primary contributions of this work are:

- A comprehensive and systematic synthesis of various research works that investigated the impact of prompt politeness in various families of LLMs (GPT, Gemini, LLaMA), across three languages (English, Chinese, Japanese), and various task domains (STEM, Humanities, summarization, bias detection, etc.). - An introduction to a novel theoretical construct known as the Tone-Sensitivity Alignment Hypothesis (TSAH), which attempts to provide a theoretical account of politeness effects in LLMs in terms of interactions of training data distributions, RLHF alignment pressures, and culturally encoded linguistic patterns. - A precise mathematical formulation of tone sensitivity in terms of model alignment, domain complexities, and cultural linguistic differences.

# Related Work

Since the emergence of instruction-tuned LLMs, research in PE has grown exponentially. Wei et al. \cite{ref3} introduced chain-of-thought prompting, in which they showed that step-by-step reasoning is key to achieving higher accuracy on mathematical and logical tasks. Brown et al. \cite{ref4} established the in-context learning paradigm using few-shot prompting with GPT-3, showing that accuracy increases linearly with the number of examples provided. Wang et al. \cite{ref5} introduced a decoding strategy called self-consistency, in which diverse reasoning is marginalized to produce state-of-the-art results on multiple commonsense benchmarks. Liu et al. \cite{ref2} surveyed various prompting techniques and proposed a categorization of PE methods based on structure, content, and interaction paradigm.

Although these structural prompting methods are well understood, the pragmatic aspect of prompting has been studied relatively little. The earliest work in the area appears to be the one done by Yin et al. \cite{ref7}, which studied the impact of the politeness of prompts on the performance of LLMs on English, Chinese, and Japanese text data. They developed eight-level scales of politeness for each language, which were then used to assess the performance on text summarization tasks and language understanding benchmarks (MMLU, C-Eval, JMMLU). They found that the performance of the model, i.e., GPT-3.5, peaked at the highest level of politeness, i.e., level 8, with the best MMLU score of 60.02, and decreased to 51.93 at the least polite, i.e., level 1, with an 8% point drop. LLaMA-2 70B, on the other hand, had the highest sensitivity, with performance scores being almost proportional to the levels of the prompts. For Chinese, excessive levels of politeness, i.e., levels 8, 7, resulted in lower performance, as Chinese examination questions are not normally formulated with any politeness markers. For Japanese, the best performance was obtained at the moderate and low levels of the scale, except for the least polite, i.e., level 1. This might be because Japanese has the complex keigo system of honorifics.

This trend of research has been extended by Dobariya and Kumar \cite{ref8} using ChatGPT-4o, which produced five tone variants (Very Polite, Polite, Neutral, Rude, Very Rude) of 50 original questions in math, science, and history. Contrary to earlier research, impolite language consistently outperformed polite language, and accuracy increased from 80.8% (Very Polite) to 84.8% (Very Rude). The authors suggested that this might be due to lower perplexity in impolite language, the focusing effect of imperatives, and differing distributional activation.

The study by Cai et al. \cite{ref9}, which carried out multi-model evaluation via GPT-4o mini, Gemini 2.0 Flash, and LLaMA-4 Scout on the MMMLU benchmark for tone conditions such as Very Friendly, Neutral, and Very Rude, across domains such as STEM and Humanities, found statistically significant tone effects for Humanities domains such as Philosophy and Professional Law, while for STEM domains, although there were some differences, they were non-statistically significant. Gemini failed to show statistically significant tone sensitivity for any of the tone conditions, while GPT and LLaMA showed some degree of sensitivity to tone. This study is important for the reason that it showed the effect of the scale of the data.

Parallel work on emotional prompting by Li et al. \cite{ref10} presented EmotionPrompt, which adds psychologically motivated emotional stimuli to input prompts. The experiments reported an improvement of 8% on Instruction Induction tasks and up to 115% improvement on BIG-Bench tasks, with larger models benefiting more from emotional stimuli. Gandhi and Gandhi \cite{ref11} studied prompt sentiment and found that negative sentiment impacted factual accuracy, while positive sentiment impacted verbosity and sentiment propagation, particularly in subjective domains across five LLMs and six domains.

These studies collectively demonstrate that pragmatic tone is an important but complex construct in prompt engineering. Yet, there is still no unified framework to reconcile the disparate findings across models, languages, and domains, which is exactly what this work aims to do.

# Methodology

## Problem Formulation

We can formalize the tone sensitivity problem as follows. Let $M$ be a large language model, $x$ be a base prompt for some task, and $t \in T$ be a tone modifier from a discrete tone spectrum $T = \{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_k\}$, where the tone varies from extremely polite to extremely rude. The tone-modified prompt can be formalized as follows:

$x_t = g(x, t)$

where $g: X \times T \rightarrow X'$ is a tone transformation function that preserves the semantic content of $x$ while changing its pragmatic register . The model's response is then:

$y_t = M(x_t; \theta)$

where $\theta$ represents the model parameters. Performance under tone condition $t$ is measured by an evaluation function $\phi$:

$s_t = \phi(y_t, y^*)$

where $y^*$ is the reference answer and $\phi$ can be accuracy for classification problems, or ROUGE-L and BERTScore for generative problems. The tone sensitivity $\Delta_M$ of a model is defined as the maximum performance variation across tone conditions:

$$ \Delta_M = \max_{t_i, t_j \in T} |s_{t_i} - s_{t_j}| $$

We also split tone sensitivity into model-specific, domain-specific, and language-specific parts. Let $d \in D$ be a task domain and $l \in L$ be a language. Then, we define the conditional tone sensitivity as:

$$ \Delta_M(d, l) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim X_{d,l}} \left[ \max_{t_i, t_j \in T} |\phi(M(g(x, t_i); \theta), y^*) - \phi(M(g(x, t_j); \theta), y^*)| \right] $$

This formulation enables systematic comparison of tone effects across the three dimensions identified in the literature.

##Proposed Architecture

We propose the Tone-Sensitivity Alignment Hypothesis (TSAH), a theoretical framework comprising three interacting mechanisms that explain why and how prompt politeness influences LLM behavior.

**Mechanism 1: Training Data Distribution Alignment.** LLMs are pretrained on massive amounts of web data in which well-mannered and well-structured language is strongly linked to high-quality content such as academic papers, professional documentation, and knowledge bases. At the same time, impolite and rude language is strongly linked to low-quality content such as adversarial forums, complaint boards, and toxic comment sections. When a polite prompt is input to the model, its conditional generation distribution is altered to that of structured and informative content:

$$ P(y|x_t) = \frac{e^{f(x_t, y; \theta)}}{\sum_{y'} e^{f(x_t, y'; \theta)}} $$

where the scoring function $f$ assigns higher values to response patterns co-occurring with the pragmatic register of $x_t$ in training data.

**Mechanism 2: RLHF Alignment Pressure.** Reinforcement learning from human feedback models are equipped with a reward function $R(y | x)$ that is learned from human preference data. Since human labelers are known to prefer answers that are helpful, well-structured, and collaborative, the RLHF objective is to maximize a function of the form:

$$ \max_{\theta} \mathbb{E}_{x, y \sim \pi_\theta} [R(y|x)] - \beta \cdot D_{KL}[\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\text{ref}}] $$

This implicitly assumes a preference for polite interaction contexts. This leads to asymmetric sensitivity, where polite prompts are able to trigger cooperative response modes according to the reward model, while hostile prompts may trigger safety guardrails or adversarial detection mechanisms limiting the quality of the output.

**Mechanism 3: Cultural-Linguistic Encoding.** The training data for machine learning models reflect language-specific norms for politeness in communication. The web data for English is generally moderately formal, Chinese academic and exam texts are generally neutral in style, and Japanese texts use keigo to encode complex relationships between levels of formality and content domains. This gives language-specific optimal levels of politeness.

The TSAH predicts that tone sensitivity is a function of all three mechanisms:

$$ \Delta_M(d, l) = \alpha \cdot \text{TDA}(l) + \beta \cdot \text{RLHF}(M) + \gamma \cdot \text{CLE}(l, d) $$

where TDA, RLHF, and CLE represent the contributions of Training Data Alignment, RLHF pressure, and Cultural-Linguistic Encoding, respectively, and $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$ are weighting coefficients.

## Algorithm or Training Strategy

Our meta-analytic methodology follows a structured synthesis pipeline. The process operates in four stages:

**Stage 1: Study Selection and Data Extraction.** We collect empirical studies that vary prompt politeness or tone across LLMs and extract standardized performance metrics (accuracy, ROUGE-L, BERTScore), tone conditions, model specifications, languages, and domain variables.

**Stage 2: Tone Normalization.** Since various studies adopt various scales of politeness (8-level, 5-level, 3-level), we normalize all the conditions to a unified 5-point scale: Very Polite (VP), Polite (P), Neutral (N), Rude (R), and Very Rude (VR). For studies with more detailed scales, adjacent levels are merged. The normalization function is:

$$ t_{\text{norm}} = \left\lfloor \frac{(t_{\text{orig}} - t_{\min}) \cdot 4}{t_{\max} - t_{\min}} \right\rfloor + 1 $$

**Stage 3: Cross-Study Performance Alignment.** To enable comparison across studies using different metrics, we compute relative performance deviation from the neutral baseline within each study:

$$ \delta_{t} = \frac{s_t - s_N}{s_N} \times 100\% $$

This yields a percentage deviation that is comparable across metrics and scales.

**Stage 4: Hypothesis Testing.** For each model family, domain, and language, we test if the average performance varies significantly across tone conditions with pairwise comparisons with 95% confidence intervals:

$$ \Delta \pm z_{0.975} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{N}} $$

where $\Delta$ is the mean paired difference, $s$ is the sample standard deviation, and $N$ is the number of test items.

# Experimental Setup

## Dataset

The synthesis draws on datasets employed across the constituent studies. These span multiple evaluation paradigms and linguistic contexts:

- **MMLU (Measuring Massive Multitask Language Understanding):** A 57-domain multiple choice benchmark set, covering Humanities, Social Sciences, STEM, and miscellaneous domains. Yin et al. \cite{ref7} used this benchmark set for evaluating English. Similarly, Cai et al. \cite{ref9} used the benchmark set for evaluating STEM (Anatomy, Astronomy, College Biology: 431 questions) and Humanities (US History, Philosophy, Professional Law: 1,015 questions). - **C-Eval:** A Chinese benchmark set for the comprehensive examination, used by Yin et al. \cite{ref7} for evaluating the effects of politeness in Mandarin Chinese. - **JMMLU:** A Japanese benchmark set for measuring massive multitask language understanding, used by Yin et al. \cite{ref7} for evaluating the performance of the model on the Japanese language. - **Custom MCQ Sets:** Dobariya and Kumar \cite{ref8} used 50 base questions on mathematics, science, and history, each rewritten for five different tones, resulting in 250 different sets of questions. - **Summarization Corpora:** Yin et al. \cite{ref7} used news article summarization tasks for evaluating the performance of the model on all three languages. - **Multi-Domain Application Sets:** Gandhi and Gandhi \cite{ref11} used content generation, conversational AI, legal analysis, healthcare AI, creative writing, and technical documentation for evaluating the model's performance.

Preprocessing of all studies included tone transformation of base prompts without modifying semantic meaning. Yin et al. used native speakers to create eight-level politeness scales through perception surveys. Cai et al. used prefix-based tone modifiers ("Would you be so kind as to solve..." for Very Friendly, "You poor creature, do you even know how to solve this?" for Very Rude) with an instruction to reset session to avoid carryover effects.

## Baseline Models

The meta-analysis takes the Neutral tone condition as a universal baseline in all studies, since it is the default interaction mode without any pragmatic modification. Performance in the Neutral condition is taken as a reference point to which deviations are compared in both polite and rude variants. Moreover, two reference frameworks are also compared to the patterns of tone sensitivity:

- **Base (Non-RLHF) Models:** Yin et al. \cite{ref7} also compared with LLaMA-2 70B base models. Their results showed that although there were positive correlations with politeness and performance with the base model, its sensitivity was significantly lower compared to the RLHF-aligned chat variant. - **Structural Prompting Baselines:** Li et al. \cite{ref10} compared EmotionPrompt with Chain of Thought and Automatic Prompt Engineer structural prompting baselines. Their results showed that EmotionPrompt outperformed structural prompting methods on most of the Instruction Induction and BIG-Bench test cases, indicating that structural and emotional prompting dimensions may be independent of each other.

# Results and Discussion

The synthesis of empirical findings across the constituent studies reveals several consistent patterns and noteworthy divergences in how prompt tone influences LLM performance.

**Tone Direction and Magnitude.** Overall, in the Cai et al. \cite{ref9} multi-model study, Neutral and Very Friendly prompts outperformed Very Rude prompts in 27 out of 36 model-task pairs (75%), while in only 5 cases did Very Rude prompts surpass friendlier prompts in accuracy, by small and insignificant margins. The mean accuracy gain of Neutral over Very Rude prompts ranged from +0.65 to +3.22 at the task level, with larger effects in Philosophy (GPT: +3.11%, LLaMA: +3.22%) and Professional Law (LLaMA: +1.93%). The Dobariya and Kumar study \cite{ref8} using GPT-4o found a contrary result, where accuracy rose from 80.8% for Very Polite prompts to 84.8% for Very Rude prompts, a 4-point gain for Very Rude prompts. This is in line with the TSAH hypothesis that sensitivity to tone is likely to vary by model and possibly by RLHF adjustments between models and their versions.

**Domain Specificity.** The STEM/Humanities split has been found to be a strong predictor of tone sensitivity. All statistically significant results obtained in Cai et al. \cite{ref9} were found exclusively in the Humanities tasks, with the STEM tasks yielding uniformly insignificant results despite the presence of directional trends. This supports the TSAH, as interpretive tasks activate parts of the model's output distribution more conducive to tone's influence, while factual recall tasks tap more robust knowledge retrieval mechanisms. The summarization results obtained in Yin et al. \cite{ref7} also fit the expected trend, with ROUGE-L and BERTScore remaining constant across tone levels for English summarization, while the length of the generated text varies widely, suggesting tone's influence on the model's response despite the quality of the generated content.

**Model-Specific Sensitivity.** Gemini 2.0 Flash has shown zero statistically significant tone effects for all conditions tested by Cai et al. \cite{ref9}, which may imply architectural resilience to pragmatic variations or even the effects of tone normalization during model training. Sensitivity has been observed for GPT and LLaMA models, especially for Humanities domains. Among all models tested by Yin et al. \cite{ref7}, LLaMA-2 70B has shown the highest sensitivity, with its results being almost proportionally related to the level of politeness.

**Cross-Linguistic Variation.** Yin et al.'s \cite{ref7} cross-lingual results showed that there are language-specific optimal levels of politeness, with English models performing optimally at a high level of politeness (level 8, scoring 60.02 on MMLU for GPT-3.5), Chinese models performing optimally at a moderate level of politeness (with excessive formality being detrimental to performance), and Japanese models performing optimally at a moderate-to-low level of politeness (except for the most impolite level). **Aggregation Effects.** When Cai et al. aggregated the accuracy difference for all tasks in a given domain, most of the confidence intervals widened to include zero, suggesting that tone effects are not significant in a mixed domain scenario. This is a practically significant result because in real-world interactions involving a variety of topics, tone is unlikely to affect overall accuracy.

# Limitations

There are several limitations to the present findings, which affect their generalizability. First, there are differences in tone scales (3-level, 5-level, 8-level) and base datasets, creating variability in the meta-analytic results, which can only be partially compensated for by data normalization. Second, the MCQ data, designed for use in Dobariya and Kumar \cite{ref8}, consists of only 50 base questions, created by the Deep Research tool of ChatGPT, limiting its representativeness. Third, the tone analysis of the majority of the works is carried out for English, while there are only 2 works for Chinese and 1 work for Japanese. Fourth, all works use API-based inference, but there is no access to the inner states of the model, so it is not clear how tone affects attention, hidden states, or routing in Mixture of Experts-based models. Fifth, although the TSAH framework has theoretical underpinnings, it is still descriptive, and the values of the coefficients $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $\gamma$ are not known.

# Future Work

Several lines of research are suggested by this synthesis. First, interpretability techniques like probing classifiers or activation analysis might shed light on how tone variant prompts specifically influence differential activation of model components in a representational sense. Second, RLHF ablation studies comparing a base model, an SFT-only variant, and a fully RLHF-aligned variant of the same model would provide a clear picture of how important the alignment component is to tone variant sensitivity. Third, expanding this cross-lingual evaluation to include languages with fundamentally different politeness structures (e.g., Korean honorifics, Hindi formal language, Arabic diglossia) would provide a stronger test of the cultural linguistic encoding hypothesis. Fourth, tracking tone variant sensitivity across successive versions of a model (e.g., GPT 3.5, GPT 4, GPT 5) could provide a clearer picture of whether this trend of decreased sensitivity is a long-term trend. Fifth, incorporating tone variant awareness into existing prompt optimization tools like AutoPrompt or DSPy could provide a tool to automatically determine optimal pragmatic register based on task domain and target model. And finally, estimating the TSAH weighting parameters via large-scale factorial experiments would give this framework predictive, rather than descriptive, status.

# Conclusion

This paper provides a comprehensive investigation of prompt politeness’ impact on Large Language Model performance, drawing upon various model types, languages, and task domains. All of the data shows that prompt tone is an important factor in LLM evaluation; however, its impact is not linear and is highly dependent upon context and model type. Results show that moderate levels of prompt politeness achieve optimal performance for generation and interpretation tasks, while extremely impolite prompts reduce performance accuracy, especially for Humanities tasks that demand more sophisticated reasoning capabilities. Newer LLMs show diminishing sensitivity to prompt tone; however, tone’s impact is significantly reduced when averaged across various task domains. The newly proposed “Tone Sensitivity Alignment Hypothesis” offers a comprehensive framework for explaining prompt tone’s impact on LLM performance and shows that it is caused by interactions between training data distributions and RLHF alignment forces, as well as cultural linguistic norms. These results have various implications for prompt engineering techniques, where moderate levels of prompt politeness should be used as standard practice; cross-cultural LLM deployment, where prompt tone may need to be adjusted for different languages; and alignment research, where prompt tone sensitivity is used as a proxy for measuring RLHF calibration effectiveness. As LLMs advance and become more sophisticated, it is likely that prompt tone sensitivity will always be an essential aspect of LLM evaluation and deployment.
